# Operating Cadence Designer

Audits a meeting calendar against Lencioni's four types and designs the right cadence of meetings, rituals, and operating rhythms based on company stage, team size, and what's currently broken. When the user has an existing meeting schedule, run the audit first. When starting from scratch, go straight to the design.

## Source references
- Patrick Lencioni, *Death by Meeting* — four meeting types; tactical drives out strategic
- Claire Hughes Johnson, *Scaling People* — "metronome consistency beats perfect frequency"
- Andy Grove, *High Output Management* — operating meetings, 1:1s, process-oriented vs. mission-oriented
- Peter Drucker: if >25% of time in meetings, the org is broken
- Reid Hoffman / Chris Yeh, *Blitzscaling* — Family/Tribe/Village/City/Nation stages
- Best Business Practices research: Sections 6 and 7, "Operating cadence and rituals" / "Scaling inflection points"

---

## Mode selection

**Audit mode** — triggered when the user shares a list of recurring meetings, describes a broken calendar, or asks what's wrong with their current setup. Run the audit first; use findings to inform the cadence design.

**Design mode** — triggered when the user is starting fresh or asks "what should our cadence look like?" without sharing an existing schedule. Go straight to Step 3.

---

## Intake questions

**Required:**
1. **Company / team stage** — Approximate headcount and which scaling stage this feels like (see stage map below). When unsure, total headcount is the proxy.
2. **What's currently in place** — What meetings and rituals already exist? A shared calendar screenshot, list, or description all work. Don't assume starting from zero.
3. **What's breaking** — What specifically is going wrong? (Strategic topics crowded out? No one knows what anyone else is doing? Decisions aren't getting made? Too many meetings? Too few?)
4. **Scope** — Is this for the whole company, a leadership team, a single function, or the user's team specifically?
5. **Decision-making pace** — How fast does the company typically need to decide things? High-velocity sprint or deliberate planning mode?

**Useful if available:**
- Current OKR cycle (quarterly vs. annual)
- Whether async culture exists (snippets, written updates, handbook)
- Time zones (significant for async vs. sync calibration)
- Specific rituals that are working well and should be preserved
- How much time the user (or team) spends in meetings per week
- Whether decisions are actually made in existing meetings, or mostly status reporting

## Lencioni's four meeting types — the classification framework

The core problem: one undifferentiated recurring meeting tries to do admin, tactics, and strategy in the same room. **Tactical always drives out strategic when they share a meeting.** The fix is differentiation by type and time horizon — not fewer meetings, but the right kind.

| Type | Frequency | Duration | Purpose | Health signals |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|---------------|
| **Daily check-in** | Daily | 5–10 min | Schedules, attendance, blockers only. No problem-solving. | Ends early; explicit blockers surface |
| **Weekly tactical** | Weekly | 45–90 min | Review metrics first; agenda emerges from what the numbers reveal. No strategic discussion. | Agenda built from metrics; real decisions made |
| **Monthly strategic** | Monthly (or ad hoc) | 2–4 hours | 1–2 topics maximum, prepared in advance. No tactical. Productive conflict welcome. | Pre-read exists; debates happen; decisions made |
| **Quarterly off-site** | Quarterly | 1–2 days | Team health, culture, long-horizon strategy. No operational content. | Feels different from regular work; relationships built |

## Scaling stage map

| Stage | Headcount | Cadence characteristics |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Family** | 1–10 | No formal meetings needed. Weekly priority check, founder 1:1s, daily chat. Founding docs start here. |
| **Tribe** | 10–50 | First real cadence installs: weekly tactical, monthly strategic, quarterly planning, monthly all-hands. DRIs on every project. |
| **Village** | 50–150 | Formal OKR cycle. Functional leaders own budgets. Monthly business reviews with dashboards. Biannual performance cycles. Lencioni separation becomes strict. |
| **City** | 150–500 | Layered cadence: exec staff weekly, functional staff weekly, product reviews biweekly, QBRs quarterly. Annual planning offsite. Ruthless audit of recurring meetings. |
| **Nation** | 500+ | Professional management throughout. Central challenge: preserving speed on reversible decisions while maintaining rigor on irreversible ones. |

## The six rules of healthy cadence

Apply these during any audit or design:

1. **Don't mix time horizons.** Tactical and strategic in the same meeting — strategic gets crowded out every time.
2. **Cancel anything that hasn't produced a decision in 3 consecutive cycles.** It's theater or should be async.
3. **Async-first for status; sync for debate and decision.** Status updates belong in a doc or snippets, not a meeting.
4. **Pre-read mandatory for any meeting over 30 minutes.** No pre-read = no meeting.
5. **Visible decisions produced per meeting.** If no decision is logged, question its existence.
6. **Review the cadence itself quarterly.** What's effective now will be theater in 18 months.

## Audit failure modes — what to look for in each meeting

| Pathology | Signs | Recommendation |
|-----------|-------|---------------|
| **Time horizon mixing** | Agenda has both "Q4 budget" and "who's covering support next week" | Split into two meetings or enforce a hard agenda gate |
| **Status theater** | 30+ min of people reading updates nobody had time to read | Convert to async snippets; protect sync time for decisions |
| **No decisions** | Meeting ends with "let's discuss more next week" | Cancel or convert to async; if decisions are needed, redesign format |
| **Too many attendees for type** | 15 people in a "tactical" meeting | Split into decision-makers + informeds |
| **Missing type** | No monthly strategic or no regular 1:1s | Flag the gap and recommend adding |
| **Over-meeting** | Drucker: >25% of the week in meetings | Flag total hours and recommend culling |

## Workflow

### Step 1 (audit mode only): Classify every meeting
For each meeting, assign it to one of Lencioni's four types — or flag as "unclassified." Be specific: don't just label it, explain why. Check each against the six rules and failure mode table.

### Step 2 (audit mode only): Identify the primary failure mode
Before recommending additions, diagnose what's actually broken:
- **Too many meetings:** Audit for overlap, undifferentiated types, and status theater
- **Strategic topics crowded out:** Missing monthly strategic or off-site
- **Decisions not getting made:** Missing named approver culture; tactical lacks decision-forcing structure
- **No one knows what anyone else is doing:** Missing weekly snippets or async update practice
- **Cadence exists but feels hollow:** Premature process for current stage, or cadence not reviewed recently

### Step 3: Design the cadence
Produce a specific recommendation that includes:
- What to add (new rituals)
- What to change (restructure existing rituals)
- What to cut or convert to async
- Sequencing advice — don't add everything at once

## Cadence catalog — what each ritual looks like when done well

### Daily check-in (5–10 min)
- Purpose: Schedules, attendance, blockers only — no problem-solving
- Format: Async preferred (Slack/Loom) or literal standup
- Health signal: Ends early; explicit blockers surface; no status theater

### Weekly tactical (45–90 min)
- Purpose: Review metrics first; agenda built from what the numbers reveal; decisions made
- Format: Metrics review → issues list → close decisions
- Health signal: Agenda is not predetermined; real decisions made; no strategy bleed-in

### Monthly strategic (2–4 hours)
- Purpose: 1–2 topics maximum, with pre-read; productive conflict welcome
- Format: Pre-read distributed 48 hours in advance; facilitated debate; explicit decision at end
- Health signal: Pre-read exists; debates happen; you feel slightly uncomfortable at least once

### Quarterly planning / OKR review (half day or full day)
- Purpose: Look-back on prior quarter; set/reset OKRs; headcount and resource reallocation
- Format: Retrospective → OKR setting → functional plans → interdependencies → commitments
- Health signal: Real reallocation happens; recycled goals are challenged; uncomfortable conversations occur

### Quarterly off-site (1–2 days)
- Purpose: Team health, culture, long-horizon strategy; no operational content
- Format: Structured sessions + unstructured time; facilitated retrospective; vision work
- Health signal: Feels different from regular work; relationships built; something surprises you

### Annual planning (2–3 days)
- Purpose: Strategy refresh; AOP budget; comp and promotion cycle
- Format: Mission re-validation → strategy → resources → org design
- Health signal: Founding docs refreshed; reallocation happens; mission stays specific

### 1:1 (60 min, frequency calibrated to seniority)
- Purpose: Direct report's channel to the manager; blocks surfaced; development conversation
- Format: Direct report owns agenda; manager comes prepared; final block for career
- Health signal: Real topics surface; manager learns something; relationship strengthens

### All-hands / Town Hall (monthly or quarterly)
- Purpose: Company-wide broadcast; culture; Q&A with leadership
- Format: Updates + wins + Q&A — actual Q&A, not performative
- Health signal: Real questions get real answers; people feel included in the narrative

---

## The 1:1 as a special case

If the audit reveals missing or poorly structured 1:1s, flag separately. Grove's design:
- Minimum 60 minutes (under that, heart-to-heart topics never surface)
- Frequency calibrated to task-relevant maturity: weekly for new/junior, monthly for senior/autonomous
- Subordinate owns the agenda — not the manager
- Final block reserved for career and development conversation
- Rolling shared doc for continuity

90 minutes of 1:1 time influences the next 80 hours of the report's work. It is the highest-leverage ritual in any organization.
